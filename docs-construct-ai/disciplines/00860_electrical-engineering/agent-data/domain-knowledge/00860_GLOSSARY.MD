# Electrical Engineering Glossary

## Key Terms

### A

**arc flash**
A dangerous electrical explosion caused by an electrical fault creating a conductive path through air. Produces extreme heat (up to 35,000°F), intense light, and a pressure wave. Governed by IEEE 1584 for hazard calculations and NFPA 70E for workplace safety requirements.

**arc-flash boundary**
The distance from an arc-flash source at which the incident energy drops to a specified level (typically 1.2 cal/cm², the threshold for second-degree burns). Used to determine PPE requirements and approach limits.

**as-built drawing**
A revised set of drawings that reflect the actual installation, including all changes made during construction. Essential for operations, maintenance, and future modifications.

**authorized employee**
A person who has been trained and designated to perform servicing or maintenance on equipment and is qualified to apply lockout-tagout procedures.

### B

**bill of quantities (BOQ)**
A document prepared by engineers or quantity surveyors that itemizes all materials, labor, and equipment required for a project, with quantities, descriptions, and units of measurement. Used for tender and procurement.

**BIM (Building Information Modeling)**
A digital 3D modeling process used by architecture, engineering, and construction professionals to design, document, and coordinate building systems. Enables clash detection between electrical, mechanical, and structural elements.

### C

**cable sizing**
Calculation of the minimum conductor cross-sectional area to carry the load current while meeting requirements for voltage drop, short-circuit withstand, ambient temperature derating, grouping derating, and thermal limits. Governed by IEC 60364 or NEC.

**Certificate of Compliance (CoC)**
A formal document certifying that an electrical installation complies with applicable codes and standards (e.g., SANS 10142-1). Required for energization, property transfers, and insurance purposes.

**clash detection**
The process of identifying spatial conflicts between building systems (e.g., cable tray intersecting HVAC duct) using BIM software. Performed continuously during design to avoid costly rework during construction.

**cold-eye review**
An independent, objective technical review of design documents or deliverables conducted by someone not directly involved in the project. Used to identify errors, omissions, and improvement opportunities before issue.

**commissioning**
The systematic process of verifying that all electrical systems and equipment operate according to design intent and owner requirements. Includes pre-functional checks, functional performance tests, and integrated systems testing.

**coordination study**
See *protection coordination*.

### D

**design basis / design philosophy**
A foundational document that defines the assumptions, standards, codes, and criteria used as the basis for electrical system design. Includes voltage levels, redundancy philosophy, earthing approach, and protection strategy.

**derating factor**
A multiplier applied to the current-carrying capacity of a cable to account for installation conditions such as ambient temperature, cable grouping, burial depth, or soil thermal resistivity.

**digital twin**
A virtual representation of a physical electrical system that contains design data, as-built information, equipment specifications, and operational parameters. Used for simulation, maintenance, and lifecycle management.

**diversity factor**
The ratio of the sum of individual maximum demands to the maximum demand of the whole system. Used in load calculations to account for the fact that not all loads operate at maximum simultaneously.

**DTR (Document Transmittal Record / Data Transmittal Record)**
A formal record of documents exchanged between parties on a project, including vendor submittals, shop drawings, and technical documentation.

### E

**earth resistance**
The total resistance of an earthing/grounding system, including electrode resistance, conductor resistance, and earth-to-soil contact resistance. Measured using earth clamp meter or fall-of-potential method. Typical targets: <1 ohm for substations, <10 ohms for general installations.

**earth fault**
A fault condition where current flows from a live conductor to earth. Detected by earth fault relays or residual current devices (RCDs).

**emergency lighting**
Lighting that automatically activates upon failure of the normal power supply to provide illumination for safe evacuation or continuation of critical operations.

**energized work**
Any work performed on or near live electrical conductors or circuit parts. Treated as an exception requiring pre-task planning, arc-flash risk assessment, approved work permits, and qualified personnel per NFPA 70E.

**equipotential bonding**
The electrical connection of conductive parts to maintain them at the same electrical potential, reducing the risk of dangerous touch voltages.

### F

**factory acceptance test (FAT)**
A test performed on electrical equipment at the manufacturer's facility before shipment to verify that the equipment meets specifications and functions correctly.

**fault current**
See *short circuit current*.

**FEED (Front-End Engineering Design)**
The preliminary engineering phase that defines the basic scope, layout, equipment sizing, and cost estimate for a project. Electrical FEED includes single-line diagrams, load lists, and equipment layouts.

### G

**group lockout**
A LOTO procedure used when multiple workers are servicing the same equipment, employing a group lockbox or hasp system to ensure all workers' locks are applied before work begins and removed only when all workers are clear.

### H

**hazardous area classification**
The division of areas where explosive gas, vapor, or dust atmospheres may be present into zones (Zone 0, 1, 2 for gases; Zone 20, 21, 22 for dust) to guide the selection of appropriately protected equipment (Ex-rated).

**high voltage (HV)**
Generally refers to voltages above 1 kV AC or 1.5 kV DC. In power systems, commonly 11 kV, 22 kV, 33 kV, 66 kV, or higher for transmission and primary distribution.

### I

**incident energy**
The thermal energy released by an arc fault, expressed in calories per square centimeter (cal/cm²), that would be impressed on a working surface at a given distance. Used to determine required PPE levels.

**insulation resistance test**
A test performed to measure the resistance of insulation between conductors and between conductors and earth. Performed using a megger (megohmmeter). Minimum values typically in MΩ.

### L

**load flow**
Analysis of power flow in an electrical network to determine voltage profiles, current magnitudes, and power losses under normal operating conditions.

**load list**
A comprehensive itemized document listing all electrical loads in a facility, including connected load, demand factor, operating characteristics, and supply requirements. Used for load calculations and equipment sizing.

**lockout-tagout (LOTO)**
A safety procedure used to ensure that equipment is properly shut off, isolated from all energy sources, and cannot be energized before maintenance or servicing is completed. Involves applying locks and tags to isolation points.

**LOTO procedure**
A documented, step-by-step process for isolating energy sources on specific equipment. Includes preparation, shutdown notification, equipment shutdown, isolation, releasing stored energy, and verification of isolation.

**low voltage (LV)**
Generally refers to voltages up to 1,000 V AC or 1,500 V DC. Common LV systems include 400/230 V (three-phase/four-wire), 480/277 V, and 208/120 V.

### M

**medium voltage (MV)**
Generally refers to voltages between 1 kV and 35 kV. Commonly used for industrial distribution and sub-transmission (e.g., 6.6 kV, 11 kV, 22 kV, 33 kV).

### N

**NFPA 70 (NEC)**
The National Electrical Code published by the National Fire Protection Association. The primary standard for electrical installation safety in the United States.

**NFPA 70E**
The Standard for Electrical Safety in the Workplace. Establishes requirements for worker protection against electrical hazards, including arc-flash analysis, PPE requirements, and safe work practices.

### O

**O&M manual (Operations and Maintenance manual)**
A comprehensive document provided by equipment manufacturers or contractors containing operating procedures, maintenance schedules, troubleshooting guides, spare parts lists, and technical data.

### P

**PME (Protective Multiple Earthing)**
See *TN-C-S system*. A type of earthing system where the neutral and protective functions are combined in a single conductor (PEN) in part of the system, as defined in IEC 60364.

**power factor**
The ratio of real power (kW) to apparent power (kVA) in an AC circuit. Low power factor increases current, losses, and equipment sizing. Corrected using capacitor banks.

**pre-commissioning**
The testing and inspection activities performed on electrical systems before energization, including insulation resistance, continuity, polarity, and protection relay testing.

**protection coordination**
The selective coordination of protective devices (relays, fuses, circuit breakers) so that only the faulty section is isolated, minimizing the impact on the rest of the power system. Also called *selective coordination*.

### R

**redundancy**
The inclusion of additional components or systems to ensure continued operation in the event of a failure. Common philosophies: N (no redundancy), N+1 (one spare), 2N (full duplicate).

**residual-energy hazard**
Stored energy in a system that can cause harm even after primary energy sources are isolated. Includes capacitors, springs, elevated loads, hydraulic/pneumatic pressure, and thermal energy.

### S

**selective coordination**
See *protection coordination*.

**short circuit current**
The very high current that flows when a fault creates a low-resistance path between phases or phase and earth. Used for protective device sizing and equipment rating verification.

**single-line diagram (SLD)**
A simplified diagram showing the electrical connections between components in a power system using single lines and standardized symbols. Used for design, analysis, and operations understanding of the power system.

**site acceptance test (SAT)**
Testing performed on electrical equipment after delivery and installation at the site to verify that it meets specifications and functions correctly in its installed environment.

**step-and-touch potential**
The voltage difference between two points on the ground (step potential) or between a grounded object and a point on the ground (touch potential) during a fault. A safety concern addressed by proper earthing design per IEEE 80.

**surge protection device (SPD)**
A device installed in electrical circuits to limit transient overvoltages caused by lightning strikes or switching operations, protecting sensitive equipment.

### T

**temporary power**
Provisional electrical power installed and used during construction. Must be properly planned, sized, and monitored to prevent overloads, circuit trips, and fire hazards.

**TN-C-S system**
An earthing system defined in IEC 60364 where the neutral and protective functions are combined in a single conductor (PEN) in part of the system and separated in the remainder. Also known as PME.

**TN-S system**
An earthing system defined in IEC 60364 where the neutral and protective functions are separate throughout the entire system.

**TT system**
An earthing system defined in IEC 60364 where the supply source is directly earthed and the exposed conductive parts of the installation are earthed independently.

**IT system**
An earthing system defined in IEC 60364 where the supply source is either isolated from earth or earthed through a high impedance, and exposed conductive parts are earthed.

### U

**UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply)**
An electrical device that provides backup power to connected equipment when the primary power source fails. Uses batteries or flywheels to maintain power during short outages and bridge to generator startup.

### V

**voltage drop**
The reduction in voltage between the source and load in an electrical circuit due to conductor resistance and reactance. Must be limited to ensure proper equipment operation. Typical limits: <4% for power circuits, <3% for lighting.
