# Civil Engineering Glossary

## Key Terms

### geotechnical investigation
Subsurface exploration to determine soil and rock properties for foundation and earthwork design. Includes boreholes, test pits, and laboratory testing.

### bearing capacity
The maximum pressure or load that soil or rock can safely support without excessive settlement or shear failure. Expressed in kPa or kN/m².

### settlement
Vertical downward movement of a structure or soil layer. Includes immediate (elastic) settlement, consolidation settlement (clay), and secondary compression.

### slope stability
The ability of a soil or rock slope to resist failure along a sliding surface. Analyzed using limit equilibrium or finite element methods.

### Rational Method
A hydrologic method for estimating peak stormwater runoff rate: Q = CiA, where C is runoff coefficient, i is rainfall intensity, and A is catchment area.

### Manning's equation
An empirical formula for calculating flow velocity in open channels and pipes: V = (1/n) × R^(2/3) × S^(1/2), where n is roughness coefficient, R is hydraulic radius, and S is slope.

### detention basin (attenuation basin)
A storage facility designed to temporarily hold stormwater runoff and release it at a controlled rate to prevent downstream flooding.

### SuDS (Sustainable Drainage Systems)
A sequence of drainage management practices designed to manage surface water runoff sustainably, mimicking natural drainage patterns.

### CBR (California Bearing Ratio)
A measure of subgrade strength used for pavement design. Higher CBR values indicate stronger subgrades.

### freeboard
The vertical distance between a design flood level (or still water level) and the top of a structure (bridge deck, levee, building floor). Typical values: 300–600 mm.

### non-conformance report (NCR)
A formal document recording work that does not meet specification or design requirements, including defect description, location, reference clause, and recommended corrective action.

### request for information (RFI)
A formal query raised during design or construction to seek clarification, resolve ambiguities, or request decisions on design matters.

### inspection and test plan (ITP)
A quality management document defining inspection hold points, witness points, and testing requirements for construction activities.

### shop drawing
Detailed drawings prepared by contractors or fabricators showing how they intend to construct or fabricate elements. Must be reviewed and approved by the design team.

### as-built records
Drawings and documents showing the final constructed configuration, incorporating all changes made during construction.

### bill of quantities (BOQ)
A document providing itemized quantities of work, materials, and labor for a construction project, used for tendering and cost management.

### cut and fill
Earthwork operations where soil is excavated (cut) from higher areas and placed (fill) in lower areas to achieve the desired ground levels.

### compaction
The process of increasing soil density by mechanical means to improve bearing capacity and reduce settlement. Typically measured as a percentage of standard Proctor or Modified AASHTO density.

### liquefaction
A phenomenon where saturated, loose granular soils lose strength and stiffness during seismic shaking, behaving like a liquid.

### dewatering
The removal of groundwater from a construction site to permit dry working conditions below the water table. Methods include sump pumping, wellpoints, and deep wells.

### pile foundation
A deep foundation element (timber, steel, or reinforced concrete) driven or bored into the ground to transfer loads to deeper, more competent soil or rock layers.

### raft foundation
A large, continuous reinforced concrete slab that distributes structural loads over a wide area, used when individual footings would be too large or when soil bearing is low.

### retaining wall
A structure designed to hold back soil or fill material and prevent slope failure. Types include cantilever, gravity, and reinforced earth walls.

### load path
The continuous route through which forces (dead, live, wind, seismic) are transferred from the point of application through structural elements to the foundations and ultimately the ground.

### exposure class
A classification (per EN 206 or equivalent) defining the environmental conditions concrete will experience, which determines required durability properties (cover, cement type, water-cement ratio).

### self-cleansing velocity
The minimum flow velocity required to prevent sediment deposition in a drainage pipe. Typically 0.6–0.75 m/s.

### time of concentration
The time required for stormwater to flow from the most hydraulically remote point of a catchment to the outlet. Used in hydrologic calculations.

### BIM (Building Information Modeling)
A digital representation of the physical and functional characteristics of a facility, supporting collaborative design, construction, and operation.

### clash detection
The process of identifying conflicts between building elements (structural, MEP, drainage) in a federated BIM model before construction.

### digital twin
A digital replica of a physical asset that is continuously updated with operational data for monitoring, analysis, and decision-making.

### critical path
The longest sequence of dependent activities in a project schedule that determines the minimum project duration. Any delay on the critical path delays the entire project.

### hold point
A designated stage in construction work beyond which work must not proceed without the approval of the designated authority (engineer, inspector, regulator).

### modified AASHTO compaction
A standard laboratory test (ASTM D1557 / AASHTO T180) for determining the maximum dry density of soils at optimum moisture content under higher compaction energy. Used for quality control of earthworks.

### factor of safety
A multiplier applied to loads (load factors) or divided into material strengths (resistance factors) to account for uncertainty and ensure structural safety. Defined by applicable codes.

### serviceability limit state (SLS)
The condition at which a structure becomes unfit for its intended use due to excessive deflection, vibration, or cracking (excluding collapse).

### ultimate limit state (ULS)
The condition at which a structure or element reaches its maximum load-carrying capacity (collapse, rupture, overturning, or failure).

### hydraulic radius
The ratio of the cross-sectional area of flow to the wetted perimeter in a channel or pipe. Used in Manning's equation.

### runoff coefficient
A dimensionless factor representing the fraction of rainfall that becomes surface runoff. Depends on surface type (impervious, grass, forest).
