# Health Domain Knowledge

## Overview

This document provides comprehensive domain knowledge for the 01400 Health discipline. It covers occupational health principles, medical surveillance programs, health incident management, fitness for work assessments, health risk assessment methodologies, and medical emergency response planning for construction and engineering environments.

---

## 1. Occupational Health Fundamentals

### 1.1 Workplace Health Hazards

Construction and engineering workplaces present a range of health hazards:

| Hazard Category | Examples | Health Effects |
|----------------|----------|----------------|
| **Chemical** | Silica dust, welding fumes, solvents, asbestos, lead, isocyanates | Respiratory disease, dermatitis, cancer, organ damage |
| **Physical** | Noise, vibration, heat stress, radiation, manual handling | Hearing loss, HAVS, heat illness, musculoskeletal disorders |
| **Biological** | Legionella, mold, sewage, animal droppings, tetanus | Infection, respiratory illness, zoonotic diseases |
| **Ergonomic** | Repetitive tasks, awkward postures, heavy lifting | MSDs, back injury, repetitive strain injury |
| **Psychosocial** | Shift work, high pressure, remote work isolation | Stress, fatigue, mental health issues |

### 1.2 Occupational Exposure Limits (OELs)

OELs define the maximum concentration of a hazardous substance in workplace air that workers can be exposed to without adverse health effects:

- **8-hour Time-Weighted Average (TWA)** — Average exposure over an 8-hour work shift
- **Short-Term Exposure Limit (STEL)** — 15-minute maximum exposure (typically 4 times the TWA)
- **Ceiling Limit** — Concentration that should never be exceeded
- **Threshold Limit Values (TLVs)** — American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH) standards
- **Workplace Exposure Limits (WELs)** — UK COSHH regulations
- **Permissible Exposure Limits (PELs)** — OSHA regulatory limits

### 1.3 Health Risk Assessment Process

1. **Hazard Identification** — Identify all health hazards in the workplace
2. **Exposure Assessment** — Measure or estimate exposure levels
3. **Risk Characterization** — Compare exposure to OELs/TLVs
4. **Control Measures** — Implement hierarchy of controls (elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE)
5. **Health Surveillance** — Monitor worker health for early detection of effects
6. **Review** — Periodic review and update of assessments

## 2. Medical Surveillance

### 2.1 Purpose

Medical surveillance is the systematic assessment of employees' health status to detect early signs of work-related ill health and prevent disease progression.

### 2.2 Surveillance Programs

| Program | Tests | Frequency | Target Population |
|---------|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Audiometry** | Pure-tone audiometry (air conduction) | Annually | Workers exposed to noise >85dB(A) |
| **Spirometry** | FVC, FEV1, FEV1/FVC ratio | 2-3 years | Workers exposed to respiratory sensitizers, dusts, fumes |
| **Biological Monitoring** | Blood/urine analysis for specific substances | Per substance half-life | Workers exposed to lead, mercury, cadmium, solvents |
| **Health Surveillance** | General health questionnaire, clinical exam | Annually | All workers in hazardous environments |
| **Dermatological** | Skin inspection | Annually | Workers exposed to skin irritants/sensitizers |
| **Hand-Arm Vibration** | HAVS assessment, cold provocation test | 2 years | Workers using vibrating tools |
| **Respiratory** | Respiratory questionnaire, lung function | 2-3 years | Workers exposed to respiratory hazards |
| **Heat Stress** | Physiological monitoring | During hot work | Workers in hot environments |

### 2.3 Audiometry

- Baseline audiogram within 3 months of employment in noisy environment
- Annual surveillance audiograms for comparison
- Standard threshold shift (STS) defined as ≥10dB average change at 2, 3, 4 kHz
- Referral criteria: STS, asymmetric hearing loss, abnormal tympanometry
- Hearing protection program integration

### 2.4 Spirometry

- FVC (Forced Vital Capacity) — Total volume exhaled
- FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second) — Volume exhaled in first second
- FEV1/FVC ratio — Indicator of obstructive vs restrictive patterns
- Predicted values based on age, height, sex, ethnicity
- Quality grading: A through F based on ATS/ERS criteria

### 2.5 Biological Monitoring

- **Lead** — Blood lead levels (BLL), ZPP (zinc protoporphyrin)
- **Mercury** — Urine mercury, blood mercury
- **Cadmium** — Urine cadmium, blood cadmium
- **Organic solvents** — Urinary metabolites (e.g., hippuric acid for toluene)
- **Isocyanates** — Urinary hexamethylene diamine (HDA)

## 3. Health Incident Management

### 3.1 Types of Health Incidents

- Occupational illness (e.g., occupational asthma, dermatitis, HAVS)
- Work-related disease (e.g., lung cancer from asbestos, noise-induced hearing loss)
- Exposure incidents (e.g., chemical splash, inhalation of toxic fumes)
- Biological exposure (e.g., needlestick injury, legionella exposure)
- Heat illness (e.g., heat exhaustion, heat stroke)
- Musculoskeletal injury (e.g., back strain from manual handling)

### 3.2 Incident Reporting Process

1. **Immediate Response** — First aid, medical treatment, scene safety
2. **Notification** — Notify supervisor, health team, HSE
3. **Investigation** — Root cause analysis, exposure assessment
4. **Medical Management** — Treatment, monitoring, follow-up
5. **Reporting** — Internal report, regulatory notification (RIDDOR, OSHA)
6. **Preventive Action** — Corrective actions, control improvements
7. **Review** — Effectiveness monitoring, trend analysis

### 3.3 Regulatory Reporting

- **RIDDOR** (UK) — Reportable diseases, dangerous occurrences
- **OSHA** (US) — Recordable illnesses, Form 300 log
- **Local regulations** — Country-specific reporting requirements

## 4. Fitness for Work

### 4.1 Assessment Types

| Assessment Type | Purpose | Timing |
|----------------|---------|--------|
| **Pre-employment** | Determine fitness for proposed role | Before job offer/start |
| **Periodic** | Monitor ongoing fitness | Annually or per risk assessment |
| **Return to Work** | Assess fitness after illness/injury | Before return to duties |
| **Health Surveillance** | Detect early signs of work-related ill health | Per surveillance schedule |
| **Functional** | Assess physical/mental capability for specific tasks | As required |
| **Drug & Alcohol** | Detect impairment | Pre-employment, random, post-incident |

### 4.2 Assessment Components

- Medical history questionnaire
- Physical examination
- Vision screening (visual acuity, color vision, depth perception)
- Hearing screening
- Lung function testing
- Musculoskeletal assessment
- Cardiovascular risk assessment
- Mental health screening
- Drug and alcohol testing

### 4.3 Fitness Categories

- **Fully Fit** — No restrictions
- **Fit with Adjustments** — Modifications to role/workplace required
- **Temporarily Unfit** — Time-limited restrictions, review date set
- **Permanently Unfit** — Cannot perform role, consider alternative employment

## 5. Health Risk Assessment

### 5.1 COSHH Assessment (UK)

Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) regulations require:

1. Identify hazardous substances
2. Assess health risks
3. Implement control measures
4. Maintain controls
5. Monitor exposure
6. Provide health surveillance
7. Inform, instruct, and train employees

### 5.2 Exposure Monitoring

- **Personal sampling** — Worker-worn sampling pumps with collection media
- **Static/area monitoring** — Fixed-point sampling for background levels
- **Real-time monitoring** — Direct-reading instruments (e.g., dust monitors, gas detectors)
- **Biological monitoring** — Analysis of biological samples

### 5.3 Hierarchy of Controls

1. **Elimination** — Remove the hazard entirely
2. **Substitution** — Replace with less hazardous substance/process
3. **Engineering Controls** — Ventilation, enclosure, automation
4. **Administrative Controls** — Rotas, procedures, training
5. **PPE** — Respiratory protection, gloves, protective clothing

## 6. Medical Emergency Response

### 6.1 Emergency Types

- Medical emergency (heart attack, stroke, anaphylaxis)
- Traumatic injury (fall from height, crush injury, amputation)
- Chemical exposure (splash, inhalation, ingestion)
- Heat illness (heat exhaustion, heat stroke)
- Biological exposure (needlestick, infectious disease)
- Mass casualty incident (multiple casualties)

### 6.2 Emergency Response Planning

1. **Risk Assessment** — Identify potential medical emergencies
2. **Resources** — First aid equipment, AEDs, first aiders, medical room
3. **Communication** — Emergency contact numbers, radio protocols
4. **Evacuation** — Casualty evacuation routes, assembly points
5. **Coordination** — Local emergency services, hospital liaison
6. **Training** — First aid training, emergency drills
7. **Review** — Post-incident review, plan updates

### 6.3 First Aid Provision

- **First Aid Risk Assessment** — Determine level of provision needed
- **First Aiders** — Number based on risk level and workforce size
- **First Aid Equipment** — First aid kits, AEDs, stretchers, oxygen
- **First Aid Room** — Dedicated medical facility for high-risk sites
- **Emergency Procedures** — Posted in prominent locations
